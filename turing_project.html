Goal of Turing Project


Through the Hans Niemann Scandal and other cheating events, I have realized a paradox: it is impossible to differentiate engine-played moves from high-level chess players' moves. Even if I used an engine, it makes it very hard for chess.com, the largest chess website, to decide if I am using an engine or not.


Nowadays, offline, officials must rely on metal detectors to prevent any cheating devices. Online, the only method of detection is through accuracy checks, time elapsed (many cheaters will take 10 seconds for every move), and abnormal rating rises.


Go world champion Ke Jie's words, I realized that there might be a new fundamental difference that targets the basis of engine moves, one that can differentiate human chess players from engines not due to accuracy but due to their own style.


I call this difference the "risk of a move."

Process

Inspiration

First

The first inspiration of the Turing Project came from Ke Jie‚Äôs quote: Playing against AI is so boring‚Ä¶ This inspired me. What makes playing against AI boring? Is it the unbeatable feeling from AI? To me, playing against an unbeatable player just does not resonate the same aura that playing against the hardest bot gave me. Additionally, even the weakest bots are not fun to play against. The engines must have a particular way of playing chess that inspires other human players to make them feel boring. There is this style of playing that makes engines different from other human.

Inspiration

Second

The second inspiration comes from my consistent reading in chess. A remark of a move immediately caught my attention: This is not the best move, but it is the move that keeps on the pressure. Skilled chess players do not always choose the best move. To win, the goal of the game is to exert pressure on opponents, and force them to make mistakes under complex situations. Style is the overall method that a chess player use to win.

Inspiration

Third

The third inspiration comes from my research in AI. As I dive deeper into AI, I realized that there is a fundamental weakness or strength that AI have: AI only accepts the chess board as input. The human player does not always focus on the chess board. A human chess player will feel his own emotions: anguish at a mistake, fearing a strong opponent, calculative on the scoreboard. A human player‚Äôs style fluctuates in real life. An AI player does not. If I could capture a portion of the style, and analyse how it changes, it can form a way to tell apart human and AI. I invented a new concept: the risk of a move.

First Version

The first version of the concept of risk uses the absolute change of the evaluation system. At a given time, if the absolute change of the evaluation after the move is large, then the move makes the game much more complex, therefore representing a intention to win.

Future Outlooks

Although risky is a good concept to identify differences between human and AI, it is not enough. Many cheaters will opt to use a hybrid of human moves and chess moves, making it even more difficult to spot them out if their skills are high. In this case, simply through comparison is not enough, as computer‚Äôs move can also be unstable during a game. The work now researched is a general solution to 100% computer choices. Further understandings of how cheater will use engines to aid their wins are needed to done a full proof of cheating.
